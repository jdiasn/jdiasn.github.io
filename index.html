Wien's law step by step

Qua 11 maio 2016

The Wein's displacement law is obtained from Planck's law. Wein's law says that the product of wavelength (of maximum emissivity) by body's temperature \(T\) is equal a constant. The figure below shows a family of emissivity curve for four values of \(T\). Hereafter, we will show how that law cam be found.

\begin{equation} \label{eq1} B(T,\lambda) = \frac{2hc^{2}}{\lambda^{5}(exp(\frac{hc}{kT})-1)} \end{equation}

By simplicity, let's define \(\alpha\equiv 2hc^{2}\) and \(\gamma\equiv \frac{hc}{kT}\)

So, the equation \ref{eq1} becomes

\begin{equation} \label{eq2} B(T, \lambda) = \alpha\lambda^{-5}(exp(\frac{\gamma}{\lambda})-1)^{-1} \end{equation}

But what we want to find? We are interested to find in which wavelength occur the maximum emissivity. For it, we need to make \(\frac{\partial B}{\partial \lambda}=0\).

\begin{equation} \begin{split} \frac{\partial B}{\partial \lambda}=\alpha\lambda^{-6}(exp(\frac{\gamma}{\lambda})-1) [-5+ \\ \gamma\lambda^{-1}(exp(\frac{\gamma}{\lambda})-1)^{-1}exp(\frac{\gamma}{\lambda})] =0 \end{split} \label{eq3} \end{equation}

The solution of equation \ref{eq3} is getting doing the factor inside of brackets equal to zero.

\begin{equation} -5+ \gamma\lambda^{-1}(exp(\frac{\gamma}{\lambda})-1)^{-1}exp(\frac{\gamma}{\lambda}) =0 \label{eq4} \end{equation}

After to reorder all factors in \ref{eq4}, we will get the equation bellow.

\begin{equation} \lambda[\exp(\frac{\gamma}{\lambda})(\frac{\gamma}{\lambda}-5)+5]=0 \label{eq5} \end{equation}

Once that \(\lambda\) is different of zero, the factor inside of brackets has to be zero.

\begin{equation} exp(\frac{\gamma}{\lambda})(\frac{\gamma}{\lambda}-5)+5=0 \label{eq6} \end{equation}

The equation \ref{eq6} is a transcendental equation, it means that this isn't possible to solve by traditional way. By simplicity, we will define \(g\equiv \frac{\gamma}{\lambda}\). So, the least equation becomes

\begin{equation} exp(g)(g-5)+5=0 \label{eq7} \end{equation}

How to solve it? One way cam be expressing exponential function by approximation using Taylor's series. An other way is to use graphical method (I will be demonstrated at other).

\begin{equation} exp(g)=1+g+\frac{g^2}{2} \label{eq8} \end{equation}

After replace \(\ref{eq8}\) in \(\ref{eq7}\); we get:

\begin{equation} (1+g+\frac{g^2}{2})(g-5)+5=0 \label{eq9} \end{equation}

After reorganize, we find

\begin{equation} g(-4-\frac{3g}{2}+\frac{g^2}{2})=0 \label{eq10} \end{equation}

Inside of brackets, we have second degree equation; then, we may conclude that \(g\) is a constant. Perhaps,

\begin{equation} g=\frac{\gamma}{\lambda}=\frac{hc}{kT\lambda}=constant \Rightarrow {\lambda}{T}=constant \end{equation}

(C.Q.D.)
